# Political Markets

## Edge Sources
1. **Poll aggregation models** — combine multiple polls with methodology weights
2. **Fundamental models** — economic indicators, incumbency, demographics
3. **Early voting data** — turnout patterns vs expected
4. **Timing arbitrage** — markets react slowly to new poll data
5. **Cross-market arbitrage** — related markets can be inconsistent

## Key Principles
- Polls have known systematic biases (response rates, likely voter screens)
- Models that account for correlated errors outperform simple averages
- Markets tend to overreact to dramatic events and underreact to slow trends
- Primary vs general election dynamics are fundamentally different

## Timing
- Far out (>3 months): fundamentals dominate, big uncertainty
- Medium (1-3 months): polling becomes more predictive
- Close (<1 month): high-quality polls are best signal
- Election night: exit polls → early returns → AP calls

## Common Pitfalls
- Don't confuse "leading in polls" with "will win" (margin of error)
- State-level vs national polls tell different stories
- Turnout is the great unknown — models vary wildly here
- "October surprises" are real but overweighted by markets
